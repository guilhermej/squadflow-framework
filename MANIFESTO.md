# The SquadFlow Manifesto

## Why this exists

Running a company is two different jobs.

**One is keeping the business alive** — closing deals every week, shipping content, answering support, processing payroll. The work never stops, and the goal is rhythm, not novelty.

**The other is changing the business** — launching a new product, migrating a system, entering a new market. The work has a beginning and an end, and the goal is delivery, not rhythm.

Most frameworks pick one and pretend the other is a special case. Scrum optimizes change into sprints. Kanban optimizes flow and calls everything a ticket. SAFe tries to do both and collapses under its own weight. Managers end up patching frameworks together with duct tape, and the result is predictable: unclear ownership, hidden work, decisions lost in Slack.

**SquadFlow is built around the distinction.** Continuous work and change work have their own shapes, their own lifecycles, their own governance. Same company, same people, different disciplines.

## Five principles

### 1. Factories run your business. Projects change it.

A **factory** is permanent. It produces the same kind of outcome over and over — sales, content, fulfillment, recruiting — and its kanban never empties. A **project** is temporary. It has a start, a scope, and an end. When a successful project produces something that must then be operated forever, it is *absorbed* into a new factory. Every other choice in SquadFlow hangs off this one.

### 2. Every entity has a single owner.

Never a committee. If no one is personally accountable for a factory, a project, a squad or a document, the thing drifts. Ownership is named and logged. An owner can delegate; an owner cannot disappear.

### 3. States are explicit.

"Done" is not a state. "In progress" is not a state. A state is one of a finite list — `backlog`, `scoping`, `active`, `delivery`, `closed` — with documented entry and exit conditions. Ambiguity about status is the leading cause of stalled work, and stalled work is silent.

### 4. Strategy and execution share one model.

OKRs are not a separate tool bolted onto the side. They are first-class citizens in the same graph as factories, projects, squads and tasks. When you ask *"which project contributes to which OKR?"*, the answer is a relation in the model, not an opinion.

### 5. Open by default.

The framework is licensed under [CC-BY-SA 4.0](LICENSE). You can use it, adapt it, translate it, build commercial products on top of it — as long as you credit the origin and keep your derivatives open under the same license. Companies running the same framework can learn from each other faster than companies running secret playbooks.

## What this is not

- **Not a replacement for Scrum or Kanban.** Those operate inside a squad or inside a factory. SquadFlow describes the layer above — where squads, factories, projects and OKRs coexist in the same organization.
- **Not a new management fad.** The ideas here are older than this document. The split between continuous operations and temporary initiatives is decades old. Squads come from the Spotify model. OKRs come from Andy Grove. SquadFlow's contribution is the rigor — a formal ontology, formal lifecycles, a formal data model — not the concepts.
- **Not a product.** There is no SquadFlow software yet. There may be one. Until then, the framework runs on Notion, on paper, or on whatever you already use.

## Who this is for

Founders, COOs, and chiefs of staff at **scale-ups and multi-brand groups** — companies large enough that ad-hoc coordination has started to break, small enough that a 200-page certification-driven framework would be overkill. If your organization has 10 to 200 people and operates more than one brand or line of business, you are the reader this was written for.
